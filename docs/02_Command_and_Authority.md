02 – Command and Authority
“Know who you answer to. Know who you speak for.”

This section defines how command and authority work within Atlas RP Emergency Medical Services (EMS), both day‑to‑day and on active scenes. It exists so every member knows who is in charge, how decisions are made, and how to escalate issues without drama.

2.1 Chain of Command
The EMS chain of command, from highest to lowest, is:

Chief

Captain

Lieutenant

Senior Paramedic

Paramedic

Advanced EMT

EMT

EMT Trainee

The following are whitelisted clinical roles that do not change the chain of command but do change who leads medical treatment in the hospital environment:

Doctor (whitelisted)

Nurse (whitelisted)

All EMS personnel are expected to:

Follow this chain of command for questions, requests, and problems.

Respect decisions from higher ranks, even when they disagree IC.

Avoid “command shopping” (asking multiple people until they get the answer they want).

If two people of the same rank are present, the one designated by command or the one who arrived first is considered in charge unless otherwise stated.

2.2 Rank Roles and Authority
Chief
The Chief is the head of the department and has ultimate authority over EMS operations.

The Chief:

Sets EMS policy and approves or vetoes SOP changes.

Approves hiring, promotions to command ranks, and long‑term suspensions/removals.

Represents EMS in server‑wide leadership discussions and events.

May override any EMS decision if it conflicts with SOP, server rules, or community direction.

Captain
The Captain is senior command and helps run the department beneath the Chief.

The Captain:

Oversees day‑to‑day operations, staffing, and major projects.

Implements policies set by the Chief and ensures they are followed.

Reviews disciplinary cases and training outcomes, recommending promotions or further action.

Acts as Chief when the Chief is offline or otherwise unavailable (if designated).

Lieutenant
Lieutenants are first‑line command and are responsible for running shifts and enforcing SOP.

Lieutenants:

Lead shifts, assign units, and manage coverage.

Handle most in‑house issues and conflicts before they reach Captain/Chief.

Can issue verbal coaching and written warnings; may recommend suspensions.

Assume scene command at major incidents when present.

Approve or deny routine requests such as LOAs, temporary role changes, and training sessions.

Senior Paramedic
Senior Paramedics are experienced medics with proven reliability and RP quality.

Senior Paramedics:

May lead smaller scenes and act as medical command when no Lieutenant+ is present.

Assist command and trainers by mentoring newer EMS members.

Are expected to model correct SOP behavior at all times.

Are not command, but their judgment on patient care decisions carries weight when no command member is available.

Paramedic
Paramedics are full ALS field providers and the core of EMS operations.

Paramedics:

Respond to calls, provide advanced treatment, transport patients, and follow SOP.

May act as scene lead when no Senior Paramedic or command member is present.

Represent the department professionally on and off scene.

Advanced EMT
Advanced EMTs are experienced BLS/ILS providers.

Advanced EMTs:

Handle higher‑acuity patients under guidance from Paramedics and above.

Assist with training EMTs and EMT Trainees when asked.

Follow direction from Senior Paramedics and command staff.

EMT
EMTs are basic field providers and the backbone of day‑to‑day response.

EMTs:

Respond to calls, provide basic treatment, transport patients, and follow SOP.

Take direction from Advanced EMTs, Paramedics, Seniors, and command staff.

Represent the department professionally on and off scene.

EMT Trainee
EMT Trainees are probationary members in training and have limited authority.

EMT Trainees:

Operate under direct or indirect supervision of an assigned trainer or senior medic.

Must ask questions and follow instructions during scenes.

Do not make major scene or policy decisions without approval.

2.2.1 Whitelisted Clinical Roles
These roles are whitelisted and granted only to trusted members who complete additional training. They do not automatically outrank anyone in the chain of command, but they do carry authority over clinical decisions in hospital settings.

Doctor (Whitelisted)
Doctors are advanced hospital medical professionals.

Doctors:

Lead advanced treatment and surgery‑style RP in hospital environments.

Direct Nurses and EMS personnel on patient care while in the hospital.

Are expected to maintain high‑quality, realistic medical RP.

Nurse (Whitelisted)
Nurses are hospital‑focused medical professionals.

Nurses:

Assist with patient care, monitoring, and post‑arrival treatment.

Support Doctors and EMS in the hospital and help keep medical RP active between calls.

May provide treatment independently within their scope when no Doctor is available.

2.3 On‑Scene Command
“On scene, clarity beats chaos.”

On any EMS‑related scene, one person should be clearly recognized as medical command.

2.3.1 Who is in Charge on Scene
Medical command typically follows this order:

Highest EMS rank (Chief, Captain, Lieutenant, Senior Paramedic, etc.) on scene acts as medical command.

If multiple of the same rank are present, the one designated by dispatch/command or the first on scene is in charge unless otherwise stated.

Whitelisted roles (Doctor/Nurse) advise on treatment but do not override the chain of command for scene control.
