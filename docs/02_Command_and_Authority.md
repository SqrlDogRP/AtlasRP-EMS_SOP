# 02 – Command and Authority

> “Know who you answer to. Know who you speak for.”

This section defines how command and authority work within Atlas RP Emergency Medical Services (EMS), both day‑to‑day and on active scenes. It exists so every member knows who is in charge, how decisions are made, and how to escalate issues without drama.

---

## 2.1 Chain of Command

The EMS chain of command, from highest to lowest, is:

1. EMS Chief  
2. Deputy Chief  
3. Captain / Lieutenant (Command Staff)  
4. Field Training Officer (FTO)  
5. Senior EMT / Paramedic  
6. EMT / Medic  
7. Probationary EMT / Trainee  

All EMS personnel are expected to:

- Follow this chain of command for questions, requests, and problems.  
- Respect decisions from higher ranks, even when they disagree IC.  
- Avoid “command shopping” (asking multiple people until they get the answer they want).

If two people of the same rank are present, the one designated by command or the one who arrived first is considered in charge unless otherwise stated.

---

## 2.2 Rank Roles and Authority

### EMS Chief

The EMS Chief is the head of the department and has ultimate authority over EMS operations.

The Chief:

- Sets EMS policy and approves or vetoes SOP changes.  
- Approves hiring, promotions to command ranks, and long‑term suspensions/removals.  
- Represents EMS in server‑wide leadership discussions and events.  
- May override any EMS decision if it conflicts with SOP, server rules, or community direction.

### Deputy Chief

The Deputy Chief is second‑in‑command and manages EMS operations when the Chief is not available.

The Deputy Chief:

- Oversees day‑to‑day operations and staffing at the department level.  
- Implements policies set by the Chief and ensures they are followed.  
- Reviews disciplinary cases and training outcomes, recommending promotions or further action.  
- Acts as Chief when the Chief is offline or otherwise unavailable.

### Captain / Lieutenant (Command Staff)

Captains and Lieutenants form EMS command staff and are responsible for running shifts and enforcing SOP.

Command staff:

- Lead shifts, assign units, and manage coverage.  
- Handle most in‑house issues and conflicts before they reach the Chief/Deputy.  
- Can issue verbal coaching and written warnings; may recommend suspensions.  
- Assume scene command at major incidents when present.  
- Approve or deny routine requests such as LOAs, temporary role changes, and training sessions.

### Field Training Officer (FTO)

FTOs are experienced medics trusted to train and evaluate new members.

FTOs:

- Supervise probationary members and ensure they understand SOP and field operations.  
- Provide feedback after calls and shifts, documenting strengths and weaknesses.  
- Recommend when a probationary member is ready to be cleared for solo duty.  
- Can temporarily pull a probationary member off scene if they are unsafe or not following instructions.

FTOs do not outrank command staff but may be treated as senior on scene regarding training matters.

### Senior EMT / Paramedic

Senior EMTs/Paramedics are medics with proven reliability and RP quality.

Seniors:

- May lead smaller scenes and act as medical command when no higher rank is present.  
- Assist command and FTOs by mentoring newer EMS members.  
- Are expected to model correct SOP behavior at all times.

Seniors are not command, but their judgment on patient care decisions carries weight when no command member is available.

### EMT / Medic

EMTs/Medics are the core of EMS operations.

They:

- Respond to calls, provide treatment, transport patients, and follow SOP.  
- Take direction from command, FTOs (when training is involved), and Seniors.  
- Represent the department professionally on and off scene.

### Probationary EMT / Trainee

Probationary members are in training and have limited authority.

They:

- Operate under direct or indirect supervision of an FTO or assigned senior medic.  
- Must ask questions and follow instructions during scenes.  
- Do not make major scene or policy decisions without approval.
